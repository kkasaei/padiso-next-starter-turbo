## The Regulatory Landscape for Health SEO in Australia

Australian health product advertising is governed by multiple bodies:

- **AHPRA** (Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency) — regulates health practitioner advertising
- **TGA** (Therapeutic Goods Administration) — regulates therapeutic claims for products
- **ACCC** (Australian Competition and Consumer Commission) — prevents misleading claims
- **FSANZ** (Food Standards Australia New Zealand) — regulates food and supplement labelling

The key rule: **you cannot make therapeutic claims about products that aren't registered as therapeutic goods**. A vitamin supplement can say "supports immune health" but not "cures colds."

## What You Can and Can't Say

### Safe Claims (Generally Permitted)
- "Supports [body function]" — "Supports digestive health"
- "Contains [ingredient] which is known to..." — factual ingredient statements
- "Helps maintain..." — maintenance rather than treatment claims
- Customer testimonials about experience (with careful framing)

### Dangerous Claims (Will Get You in Trouble)
- "Cures," "treats," "prevents" any disease or condition
- "Clinically proven" without actual clinical trial data
- Before/after comparisons implying treatment outcomes
- Medical diagnoses or specific health condition solutions

## How AI Produces Compliant Content

AI can be trained with compliance rules to generate safe content at scale:

### Prompt Engineering for Compliance
Include explicit rules in every content generation prompt:
- "Do not make therapeutic claims. Use 'supports' and 'helps maintain' instead of 'treats' or 'cures.'"
- "Reference ingredients and their general properties, not specific health outcomes."
- "Include a general health disclaimer at the bottom of every product description."
- "If uncertain about a claim, use softer language or omit the claim entirely."

### Content Review Pipeline
Even with compliance-trained AI, review is essential:
1. AI generates content with compliance rules
2. Automated compliance scanning flags risky language
3. Human review by someone familiar with TGA/AHPRA guidelines
4. Legal review for high-risk product categories

## SEO Strategy for Health and Supplements

### Ingredient Education Content
This is your safest and highest-value content type:
- "What is [ingredient]?" — purely educational, no product claims
- "How [ingredient] works in the body" — scientific explanation
- "[Ingredient] food sources and supplementation" — informational
- "[Ingredient A] vs [Ingredient B]" — comparison without therapeutic claims

### Lifestyle and Wellness Content
Content that positions products within a healthy lifestyle:
- "Morning wellness routine ideas" — products featured as lifestyle choices
- "Post-workout recovery tips" — supplements mentioned as part of recovery
- "Winter immunity support checklist" — general wellness, not treatment

### Product-Focused Content (Careful!)
Product pages and descriptions need the most careful compliance:
- Focus on ingredients, dosage, and quality certifications
- Reference TGA listing numbers where applicable
- Include required warnings and disclaimers
- Use benefit language tied to ingredients, not disease claims

## Schema Markup for Health Products

Proper schema helps Google understand your products:
- Product schema with detailed ingredient lists
- No schema properties that imply medical use (unless TGA registered)
- Review schema from verified purchasers
- FAQ schema with compliant Q&A content

## FAQs

- **Can AI really understand AHPRA regulations?**
AI can follow compliance rules embedded in prompts. It won't replace legal review, but it dramatically reduces the volume of non-compliant content generated.

- **What happens if my content violates TGA guidelines?**
Penalties range from warning letters to fines and product deregistration. It's not worth the risk—invest in compliant content from the start.

- **Should I avoid SEO for health products entirely?**
Absolutely not. Educational, ingredient-focused content is both compliant and high-value for SEO. The opportunity is massive if done correctly.
